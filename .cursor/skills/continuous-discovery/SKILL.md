# Continuous Discovery Habits

> A practice framework by Teresa Torres for making customer contact a sustainable weekly habit. This skill covers the **how** of talking to customers regularly — recruiting, interviewing, synthesizing, and assumption testing. For structuring what you learn into an actionable tree, see the `opportunity-solution-tree` skill.

## Core Principle

Discovery is not a phase. It runs in parallel with delivery — always. Teams that treat discovery as a gate ("first we research, then we build") will never sustain it. The goal is to make discovery so lightweight that skipping it feels harder than doing it.

**Minimum viable cadence**: one customer interview per week, conducted by the Product Trio (PM + Designer + Engineer) together.

## 1. Set Up Automated Recruiting

The biggest friction to weekly interviews is finding participants. Remove that friction entirely by automating recruitment so interviews appear on the calendar without the product team lifting a finger.

### Strategy A: In-Product Intercept (Consumer / B2B End Users)

Embed a short prompt inside the product (like an NPS survey, but instead of "Would you recommend us?" it asks "Do you have 20 minutes to talk to us?"). When the user says yes, send them scheduling software (Calendly, Google Calendar Appointments, or equivalent). They pick a slot. Done.

Refinements over time:
- Control **where** in the product the prompt appears (target moments of friction or recent feature usage).
- Control **who** sees it (new users, power users, churned users — rotate weekly).
- Position it as a conversation, not a survey.

### Strategy B: Leverage Internal Teams (B2B Buyers / Decision-Makers)

Sales, account management, and support teams talk to buyers daily. Ask them to recruit on your behalf.

Weekly trigger: send a one-line brief — "This week we want to talk to someone who recently experienced [specific situation]." When the internal team encounters that person, they use the same scheduling link to book the interview.

The product team's only job: show up.

### Strategy C: Personal Network (Getting Started / No Access)

If the team has zero infrastructure and "isn't allowed" to talk to customers:
- Find people in personal networks who resemble the target user.
- Consumer products: almost anyone qualifies. Just start.
- B2B / niche: ask teammates — "Do you know a nurse/doctor/accountant?" Often one team member knows someone directly.

This is not ideal long-term, but it removes the excuse. One conversation is better than zero.

## 2. The Story-Based Interview Technique

Most interviews fail because they ask direct, decontextualized questions. Human memory is unreliable when disconnected from a specific moment. Story-based interviewing grounds the conversation in real past behavior.

### The Opening Question

Use exactly one prompt to launch the interview:

> "Tell me about the last time you [relevant activity]."

Examples:
- "Tell me about the last time you watched something on a streaming service."
- "Tell me about the last time you tried to file an expense report."
- "Tell me about the last time you onboarded a new hire."

### Follow the Timeline

After the opening, the interviewer's only job is to walk through what happened chronologically:

1. **Set the scene** — "Where were you? Who were you with? What time of day was it?"
2. **Advance the timeline** — "What happened next?" (repeat as needed)
3. **Dig into friction** — "You mentioned you couldn't find anything. Tell me more about that."
4. **Summarize and redirect** — Reflect what you heard, then return to a moment that deserves more depth.

You can run an entire 20-minute interview with just the opening question plus "What happened next?" and "Tell me more about that."

### What Makes a Good Interview

| Signal | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| Feels like having a beer with a friend | Conversational cadence is right |
| Customer says "When can we do this again?" | You listened well and they enjoyed it |
| You learned something you didn't think to ask about | The story revealed hidden needs |
| You can visualize the customer's full experience | You collected enough detail |

### The Behavior Rule

Focus exclusively on **what the customer actually did** — never on hypotheticals.

| Ask This | Not This |
|----------|----------|
| "Tell me about the last time you..." | "What would you do if..." |
| "What happened next?" | "What do you usually do?" |
| "How did you decide that?" (in context of a specific story) | "Why do you prefer X over Y?" |
| "Walk me through what you saw on screen." | "What features do you wish existed?" |

People are excellent at answering hypothetical questions — but those answers don't reflect real behavior. Opinions don't predict actions. Stories do.

## 3. Common Interview Mistakes

### Mistake 1: The 50-Question Protocol

Writing a long list of who/what/why/how questions breaks the conversational flow. The interviewer becomes so focused on the next question that they stop listening. The cadence turns into an interrogation instead of a conversation.

**Fix**: Prepare one opening question and 2–3 areas of curiosity. Let the story guide you.

### Mistake 2: Staying Shallow

The interviewer hears something interesting ("I opened Netflix, didn't find anything") and immediately moves on ("Great, tell me another story"). All the value is in the details — what did they look at? What didn't resonate? Why did they give up?

**Fix**: When the customer describes any moment of friction or decision-making, slow down. Ask "Tell me more about that." Dig in before moving forward.

### Mistake 3: Collecting Facts Instead of Stories

"What do you like to watch?" produces a fact ("action movies"). You can't visualize the experience. You can't hear unmet needs. You just have a label.

**Fix**: Always anchor in a specific instance. "Tell me about the last time you chose an action movie to watch" gives you the full context.

### Mistake 4: Asking About Opinions and Preferences

"Do you think this feature is useful?" invites rationalization. The brain produces confident answers that don't match behavior.

**Fix**: Redirect to behavior. "Show me the last time you used this" or "Tell me about a time when you needed to do X."

### Mistake 5: Fear of Bothering Customers

Sales teams resist connecting product teams with customers, worried about "asking a favor." In reality, when interviews are story-based and conversational, customers enjoy them.

**Fix**: Reframe the ask. It's not a survey or a favor — it's a 20-minute conversation where they share their experience. People like talking about themselves when someone genuinely listens.

## 4. The Product Trio

Discovery works best when three perspectives collaborate:

| Role | Brings |
|------|--------|
| **Product Manager** | Business context, outcome clarity, prioritization |
| **Designer** | User empathy, experience framing, solution exploration |
| **Software Engineer** | Feasibility awareness, technical opportunity spotting |

All three attend interviews together. All three participate in synthesis and solution ideation. Decisions are made collaboratively — not by the PM as "CEO of the product."

When the trio works from shared understanding (same interviews, same synthesis), disagreements drop dramatically. When disagreements remain, it signals they haven't found the best option yet — keep exploring.

Teams that have never experienced a well-functioning trio often find this idea unrealistic. Start small: invite one engineer to one interview. Let the value speak for itself.

## 5. Synthesizing Interview Learnings

### Hear Opportunities, Not Solutions

While listening to stories, train your ear for **unmet needs, pain points, and desires** — not feature requests.

Customers are so accustomed to mediocre experiences that they often aren't consciously aware of their needs. Stories surface what direct questions cannot. See the `opportunity-solution-tree` skill for how to frame and structure these opportunities.

### After Each Interview

1. **Debrief immediately** (trio together, 5–10 minutes).
2. Capture 3–5 specific opportunities heard in the story.
3. Frame each as a customer need, not a solution ("It's hard to enter my password using the on-screen keyboard" — not "add voice input").
4. Place opportunities on the team's Opportunity Solution Tree (see `opportunity-solution-tree` skill).

### Pattern Recognition Across Interviews

Over weeks, the same needs will surface from different customers in different stories. This convergence is the signal that an opportunity is real and worth pursuing. Don't wait for large-sample statistical significance — continuous discovery works on small data because you have continuous feedback loops (delivery metrics, more interviews, assumption tests).

## 6. Assumption Testing

Once the team selects an opportunity and generates solution ideas, the next step is not "build it and see." Break each solution into underlying assumptions and test the riskiest ones first.

### How It Works

1. Take a solution idea and list every assumption that must be true for it to succeed.
2. Prioritize: which assumption, if wrong, kills the idea?
3. Design the smallest possible test for that single assumption.
4. Run 6–12 assumption tests per week across ~3 candidate solutions.
5. At the end of the week, compare which solutions have the strongest foundation.

### Key Insight

Assumption testing IS the beginning of delivery. There is no clean boundary between "discovery" and "delivery." A prototype test, a fake-door experiment, a concierge test — they all produce learning AND move the product forward.

Teams stuck in project-based research run experiments that take weeks. Continuous discovery teams run half a dozen small tests in a single week, spanning multiple solutions. That's what makes the cadence sustainable.

## 7. Overcoming "We Don't Have Time for Research"

This objection usually means "we don't have time for project-based, multi-week research studies." Correct — and that's not what continuous discovery asks for.

### The Reframe

| Old Mindset | Continuous Discovery Mindset |
|-------------|------------------------------|
| Discovery is a phase before delivery | Discovery and delivery run in parallel, always |
| We need to fully discover before building | Everything in the backlog is a bet — discovery makes bets better, it doesn't replace them |
| Research takes weeks | One interview per week, 20 minutes |
| We need permission and proper channels | Start with your personal network today |
| Small sample sizes are unreliable | One is better than zero. Feedback loops compound over time |

### Practical Starting Steps

1. **Find one person to talk to this week.** Anyone who resembles your user. Personal network is fine.
2. **Ask one story question.** "Tell me about the last time you [did the thing our product helps with]." Follow the timeline.
3. **Debrief with your trio.** What needs or pain points did you hear?
4. **Repeat next week.** Consistency matters more than perfection.
5. **Set up automated recruiting** (in-product intercept or internal team triggers) within the first month.
6. **Keep delivering in parallel.** Never pause delivery to "do discovery." The bets you're shipping today get better as your discovery habit compounds.

### The Compounding Effect

Week 1: one interview feels awkward and produces modest insight. Week 8: the trio has deep intuition about the opportunity space. Week 20: bets are noticeably sharper. The habit compounds — but only if it's continuous.

## 8. Discovery at Any Company Stage or Size

The fundamental unit — a trio with an outcome, interviewing weekly, testing assumptions — works at any scale.

| Stage | What Changes |
|-------|-------------|
| 3-person startup | No adjacent teams. Full autonomy. Discovery is survival. |
| Growth stage | More teams. Need lateral collaboration to keep product coherent. |
| Enterprise (100k+) | Design systems, dependencies, cross-team coordination. But the base unit stays the same. |

### Individual Contributors in Feature Factories

Even when prescribed a fixed roadmap:
- You still make dozens of small decisions daily. Discovery makes those decisions better.
- You don't need organizational permission to understand your business model and your customer's needs.
- Focus on developing the habit yourself. Don't try to force organizational change from an IC role — just work differently and let results speak.

---

## 9. Defending Small-Sample Discovery

When stakeholders challenge decisions based on "just a few interviews":

1. **Flip the question.** "How many customers did you talk to before making your last decision? One is better than zero."
2. **Explain the feedback loop.** Product teams are not seeking academic truth — they're making bets with continuous correction. Interview → build → measure → interview again.
3. **Continuous, not conclusive.** Each interview adds signal. Over weeks, patterns emerge and confidence grows. It's not one interview deciding everything — it's a compounding habit.

## When to Use This Skill

- A team wants to start doing customer interviews but doesn't know where to begin.
- Someone says "we don't have time for research" — reframe to continuous cadence.
- Preparing an interview guide — coach the story-based technique.
- Reviewing interview questions that are too direct or opinion-based.
- Setting up an automated recruiting system.
- Coaching a product trio on how to do discovery together.
- Synthesizing learnings from interviews into actionable opportunities (bridge to `opportunity-solution-tree` skill).
- Helping teams that do discovery as a one-time phase shift to a continuous practice.

## Agent Guidelines

When helping someone adopt continuous discovery:

1. **Start with the habit, not the framework.** Getting one interview on the calendar matters more than a perfect OST.
2. **Coach the story question.** If the user shares interview questions, check if they're story-based. Rewrite direct questions into "Tell me about the last time..." format.
3. **Remove recruiting friction.** If the user says they can't find participants, brainstorm the three strategies (in-product intercept, internal teams, personal network).
4. **Never suggest pausing delivery.** Discovery runs alongside delivery. Both always. Say this explicitly.
5. **Push for specificity in opportunities.** If synthesis produces vague needs ("easier to use"), coach toward specific, situated friction.
6. **Recommend the trio.** If only the PM is doing interviews, suggest inviting a designer or engineer to the next one.
7. **Bridge to the OST.** Once the team is interviewing regularly, point them to the `opportunity-solution-tree` skill for structuring what they learn.
8. **Normalize small data.** One interview per week is the floor, not the ceiling — but it's infinitely better than zero.
