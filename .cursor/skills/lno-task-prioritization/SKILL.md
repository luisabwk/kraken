# LNO Task Prioritization

Classify every task as L, N, or O — then match your energy and perfectionism to the task type. Stop treating all work equally.

> "All your tasks are not created equal. There are actually three types of tasks."
> — Shreyas Doshi

## When to Use

- Weekly/daily planning sessions
- When feeling overwhelmed or "busy but unproductive"
- Sprint or quarterly planning
- When procrastinating on important work
- When perfectionism is slowing output
- Coaching someone on time management

## The Three Task Types

### L — Leverage Tasks

**1x effort → 10x–100x impact**

These are the tasks where quality and depth compound into outsized returns. Your perfectionism is an asset here.

**Characteristics:**
- High strategic importance
- Outcomes affect many people, teams, or customers
- Often ambiguous and require deep thinking
- Frequently procrastinated on (because they're hard and scary)
- You deep down know you should be doing them

**Common examples for PMs:**
- Writing the product strategy document
- Crafting a PRD for a high-stakes feature
- Aligning two misaligned teams through difficult conversations
- Preparing post-CEO-review notes on a contentious decision (getting the language exactly right)
- Filing a detailed, annotated bug report for a critical customer-facing issue
- Building the business case that changes the roadmap

**How to execute:**
- Schedule during peak energy hours
- Block dedicated, uninterrupted time
- Let your inner perfectionist shine — this is where it pays off
- Change physical location if possible (coffee shop, coworking space)
- Spend 2x–3x the time you normally would

### N — Neutral Tasks

**1x effort → 1x–1.5x impact**

You get roughly what you put in. Do these competently but don't over-invest.

**Characteristics:**
- Important but not transformative
- Clear scope and expectations
- Standard recurring work
- Diminishing returns on additional polish

**Common examples for PMs:**
- Routine status updates
- Standard sprint planning facilitation
- Filing a bug report for a non-critical issue (one screenshot, basic description, submit)
- Responding to stakeholder questions with known answers
- Writing standard meeting notes (list action items, owners, hit send)
- Updating project trackers

**How to execute:**
- Do competently, don't gold-plate
- Batch similar N tasks together
- Use templates and shortcuts
- Time-box strictly
- Good enough is good enough

### O — Overhead Tasks

**1x effort → 0.1x–0.5x impact**

Necessary organizational friction. The return is always less than the effort. Minimize time here.

**Characteristics:**
- Administrative necessities
- Process compliance
- Low creative or strategic value
- Often feel "productive" but aren't (placebo productivity)

**Common examples for PMs:**
- Expense reports
- Filling out planning templates that nobody reads
- Routine approvals and sign-offs
- Formatting documents for standardized presentations
- Scheduling meetings
- Tool/system administration

**How to execute:**
- Minimum viable effort
- Automate or delegate where possible
- Do not let perfectionism anywhere near these
- Batch and blast through them
- Accept imperfection without guilt

## Critical Insight: Same Activity, Different Classification

The **same type of task** can be L, N, or O depending on context. This is the most commonly missed nuance.

| Activity | When it's L | When it's N | When it's O |
|----------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| **Filing a bug** | Critical customer-facing bug blocking a launch — detailed, annotated, 15 screenshots | Standard bug — fill the template, one screenshot, submit | Cosmetic issue in an internal tool — quick note, no template |
| **Taking notes** | CEO product review on a contentious decision — perfect the language, clarify the decision, prevent misinterpretation | Regular team sync — list the 3 action items and owners, hit send | Internal brainstorm with no decisions — quick bullet points or skip entirely |
| **Writing a PRD** | New product line that will define the next 2 years | Feature enhancement with clear requirements | Minor copy change that just needs a ticket |
| **Sending an email** | Cross-functional alignment email that 50 people will reference | Routine update to your manager | Auto-forwarding a report |

## Why We Fail at This

### The Perfectionist Trap

Ambitious people treat all tasks the same. They try to do everything at L-level quality. Result: they spend 2 hours on an expense report and 30 minutes on a strategy doc.

### The Procrastination Pattern

L tasks are **the tasks you know you should do but keep avoiding**. Common excuses:

- "I don't have time — I'm firefighting" (doing N and O tasks instead)
- "Next month will be calmer" (it won't be)
- "I need more data before I can start" (you don't)

The real reasons for procrastination on L tasks:
1. **Fear**: Deep down, you're not sure you can produce something good enough
2. **Ambiguity**: L tasks require dedicated attention with unclear paths
3. **Discomfort**: Difficult conversations, hard tradeoffs, exposing your thinking

> "Pay attention to your fears because they're telling you something."
> — Shreyas Doshi

### Placebo Productivity

Filling your day with N and O tasks *feels* productive. You're busy, you're checking boxes, you're responsive. But impact is near zero. Shreyas calls this **placebo productivity**.

## Tactical Application

### Daily Workflow

1. **Morning (peak energy):** Identify and attack your L task for the day. One is usually enough.
2. **Post-lunch / low energy:** Batch N tasks. Execute competently, don't over-invest.
3. **End of day / gaps between meetings:** Blast through O tasks. Minimum viable effort.

### Weekly Planning

```markdown
## Weekly LNO Classification

### L Tasks (Leverage) — Deep focus, peak energy
- [ ] [Task] — Why it's L: [brief reason]
- [ ] [Task] — Why it's L: [brief reason]

### N Tasks (Neutral) — Competent execution, time-boxed
- [ ] [Task]
- [ ] [Task]

### O Tasks (Overhead) — Minimum viable effort
- [ ] [Task]
- [ ] [Task]
```

### Overcoming L-Task Procrastination

Shreyas shares two tactics that work:

#### Tactic 1: Deliberate Placebo Productivity

Intentionally do N and O tasks for 1–2 days leading up to your L task. Keep reminding yourself: "I'm doing placebo productivity right now." This builds psychological pressure that makes you *want* to finally tackle the L task.

#### Tactic 2: Change of Location

On the day you need to do your L task, physically move to a different workspace — a coffee shop, coworking space, a different room. The change of environment forces a mental gear shift and fights procrastination more effectively than willpower alone.

### Quarterly Planning Allocation

Use Shreyas' allocation guidance when planning your team's quarter:

| Category | % of Capacity | Description |
|----------|---------------|-------------|
| **Incrementals** | ~60% | High-ROI improvements to existing product (N work) |
| **Big new initiatives** | ~30% | 1–2 transformative bets (L work) |
| **Stability & infrastructure** | ~10% | Tech debt, reliability (necessary O work) |

The 30% for big initiatives prevents the team from filling the entire roadmap with safe, incremental work (positive ROI but low opportunity cost).

## Complementary Framework: Opportunity Cost over ROI

From the same episode, Shreyas makes a related point about prioritization:

- **ROI mindset** (suboptimal): "Is this a good use of my time?" → Leads to filling your plate with quick wins because small-denominator tasks inflate ROI.
- **Opportunity cost mindset** (optimal): "Is this the **best** use of my time?" → Forces you to compare against the highest-impact option available.

In a high-leverage role, hundreds of tasks have positive ROI. The question isn't whether something is worth doing — it's whether it's worth doing *instead of the best alternative*.

**Practical rule:** Before committing to any initiative, ask: "What are we **not** doing by choosing this?" If the answer includes something clearly more impactful, reconsider.

## Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | Fix |
|-------------|-------------|-----|
| Treating all tasks as L | Burnout, no time for actual L tasks | Consciously downgrade N and O quality |
| Perfectionism on O tasks | Wasted energy on zero-leverage work | Set a hard time limit (e.g., 15 min max) |
| Avoiding L tasks entirely | Highest-impact work never gets done | Use placebo productivity + location change |
| Only doing "quick wins" | ROI looks great, but opportunity cost is massive | Ask "Is this the *best* use of my time?" |
| Not re-classifying tasks | Static classification ignores context changes | Re-evaluate weekly — context determines L/N/O |
| Scheduling L tasks during low energy | Poor quality on the work that matters most | Protect peak-energy blocks for L tasks only |
| Filling the roadmap with incrementals | Safe but transformative opportunities are missed | Enforce a % allocation for big bets |

## LNO Decision Quick Reference

When classifying a task, ask:

1. **Would doing this at exceptional quality change outcomes 10x?** → L task
2. **Would doing this at exceptional quality change outcomes 1–1.5x?** → N task
3. **Would doing this at exceptional quality change outcomes barely at all?** → O task

Then match your effort:

| Task Type | Effort Level | Perfectionism | Energy Required | Time Investment |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **L** | Maximum | Encouraged | Peak hours | 2x–3x normal |
| **N** | Standard | Controlled | Normal hours | 1x normal |
| **O** | Minimum | Forbidden | Off-peak / gaps | 0.25x–0.5x normal |
