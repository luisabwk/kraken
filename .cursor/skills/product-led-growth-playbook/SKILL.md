# Product-Led Growth Playbook

Elena Verna's strategic frameworks for B2B growth, extracted from her appearances on Lenny's Podcast. Elena has led growth at Miro, Amplitude, Dropbox, SurveyMonkey, Lovable, and advised dozens of companies including Superhuman, MongoDB, Netlify, and others.

Core thesis: **Growth amplifies great product-market fit. It cannot create it, fix it, or reverse its decline.**

---

## Growth Readiness Assessment

Before recommending any growth initiative, evaluate the company against these prerequisites:

### Two Prerequisites for a Growth Team

| Prerequisite | Test | Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| **Product-market fit** | Retention is healthy; users solve their problem AND stay | Sean Ellis score, cohort retention curves |
| **Data volume** | Enough users to run experiments and derive patterns | If your users fit in a spreadsheet, you don't need growth |

**Rule**: Do not hire a growth team until both are met. First wave of growth MUST be founder-led.

### When to Hire Growth vs Sales (First)

| Your monetization model | Hire first | Growth team timing |
|---|---|---|
| Sales-led (enterprise contracts) | Sales | Wait until overlaying PLG on top of sales motion |
| Self-serve (product sells itself) | Growth | Before sales; sales will be opportunistic |
| Hybrid (product acquires, sales closes) | Both early | Growth slightly before or alongside sales |

Companies like Notion hit $10M ARR before hiring their first salesperson (support team closed deals). Miro hit $5-7M ARR the same way. The longer the founder leads growth, the more the entire company learns to own it.

## Anti-Patterns (Red Flags)

These are the most common growth mistakes. If you see any on a roadmap, flag them immediately.

### 1. Hiring Growth Too Early

**Pattern**: Startup at <$1M ARR hires "Head of Growth" to figure out distribution.

**Why it fails**: Growth cannot find PMF. A shiny resume cannot wave a magic wand. Growth teams need data to form hypotheses and run experiments. 10 users is a spreadsheet, not a dataset.

**Correct approach**: Founder-led growth until PMF is solid and there's enough data volume to experiment.

### 2. Hiring Growth to Reverse Decline

**Pattern**: Business is declining. Leadership hires a growth head expecting them to create a J-curve reacceleration.

**Why it fails**: If the core product or marketing has fundamental issues, growth can only optimize by 10-15% at best. Growth amplifies trajectory — it cannot reverse it. Head of growth is destined to fail; firing cycle repeats.

**Correct approach**: First, stop the decline. Core product and marketing teams must at least plateau the metrics. Only then bring growth to amplify the upward trajectory.

### 3. Rebrand / Homepage Redesign to Drive Growth

**Pattern**: New CMO arrives, redesigns website to their personal taste, promises acquisition lift.

**Why it fails**: Elena has never seen a rebrand or marketing site redesign produce positive performance results at launch. Best case: net neutral. Every rebrand is a step BACK in performance. It resets all prior optimization.

**What actually happens**:
- $1M+ agency spend
- 8-10 months of development arguing about shades of blue
- Lackluster results at launch
- Fire drill to fix the performance regression

**If you must rebrand**: Accept the step back. Budget 3-6 months of post-launch optimization. Never promise acquisition lift. Frame it as unlocking a new global maxima that requires hard work to reach.

### 4. Obsessing Over Competition / Blind Copying

**Pattern**: "Company X is successful. Let's copy their onboarding flow."

**Why it fails**: You don't know if you're seeing their actual experience or a test variant. You don't know if it performs well for them. At Dropbox, someone wanted to copy a page that hadn't been touched in 10 years and "performed like shit."

**Correct approach**: Use competition for INSPIRATION, never as destination. Build Miro boards of competitors' flows and extract common patterns, but never skip ideation, user research, and experimentation. Copying competition is the fastest way to mediocrity.

**Also dangerous**: Benchmarks. Metric definitions differ wildly between companies (what counts as a "signup"?). Use benchmarks as a directional input, never as a target.

### 5. Over-Experimenting (Paralysis by A/B Test)

**Pattern**: Every single initiative requires a statistically significant experiment before shipping.

**Why it fails**: Paralyzing disease that kills velocity, learning speed, and team morale. Low-traffic areas may take 8 months to reach sample size.

**Rule of thumb**: If you cannot collect the sample size within ONE MONTH, don't A/B test it. Use pre/post analysis instead. Do 24-hour, 7-day, and 28-day readouts. Come back at 1 year for retention data.

**When TO experiment**: High-traffic surfaces where 0.1% matters (millions in revenue impact), or major strategic pivots that need validation.

### 6. Prioritizing Rented Channels Over Earned Channels

**Pattern**: Growth team spends 100% of effort on SEO, SEM, paid social.

**Why it fails**: You're making Google/Meta richer. Algorithm can giveth and taketh away at any point. Competitors can outbid you. You own nothing.

**Correct approach**: Growth team's #1 priority is building EARNED channels — virality, word of mouth, user-generated content. At Dropbox, 50%+ of acquisition came through sharing. That's an earned channel nobody can compete with.

**Allocation**: Diversify. Don't go 100% rented OR 100% earned. But earned channels must be on the roadmap.

### 7. Quick-Fire Anti-Patterns

| Anti-pattern | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| **Testing button colors** | A blue is a blue. Early 2000s tactic. Pick a color and move on. |
| **Third-party auth for acquisition** | Won't drive more signups. It's UX, not growth. Exception: GitHub auth for dev tools. |
| **One-off email campaigns** | 25% open rate, tiny click rate. One email never moves anything. Think in series and strategy. |
| **"Simplify onboarding" as a goal** | Simplifying is a solution, never a problem. The real problem is confusion, cognitive load, or lack of education. |
| **Thinking your problem is unique** | 99% chance someone has solved it. Find them on LinkedIn, ask. Hire advisors. Don't reinvent. |

## Growth Motion Decision Framework

### PLG vs Sales-Led vs Product-Led Sales

```
                    Individual          Team              Enterprise
                    Use Case            Use Case          Value Prop
                    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
Product-Led         Self-serve          Virality /        Self-serve
Growth              activation          collaboration     monetization
                                                          (cap ~$10K)
                    ─────────── PLS Bridge ──────────────────────────
Product-Led         Product creates     PQA signals       Sales closes
Sales               pipeline            trigger sales     $15K-$100K+
                                                          contracts
                    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sales-Led           Marketing creates                     Sales sells
Growth              pipeline (MQLs)                       top-down
```

### When to Add Product-Led Sales

| Starting from | Direction | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| PLG company | Going upmarket | Organic demand from users wanting enterprise contracts. Hand-raisers appear in support channels. |
| Sales-led company | Going downmarket | Fixed cost of sale doesn't scale. Need automation. Customers want to see value before signing. |

**Critical rule**: Do NOT hire any salespeople until you feel organic pull from users wanting to buy. Notion and Miro closed early deals through support teams.

### PQA Model (Product-Qualified Accounts)

PQA replaces gut-feel sales targeting with usage-based signals:

| Signal type | Examples | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **User count** | 7+ users in account | Magic number ~7 (parallels Facebook's 7 friends, LinkedIn's 7 connections) |
| **Volume threshold** | Events sent (Amplitude), boards created (Miro), storage used (Dropbox) | Shows depth of adoption |
| **Velocity change** | 1 user/day → 15 users/day | Most powerful timing signal but hardest to measure |
| **Behavioral signals** | Admin switch, terms-of-use page visit, pricing page view | Universal across companies; terms-of-use visits = buyer evaluating |

**Start manually**. Intuition + sales feedback first. Simple regression model second. Scale with tooling only after you understand the data. Buying a platform won't bridge the gap of understanding your data.

### Three Pipeline Sources in PLS

1. **Usage + PQL exists**: User in account is also the buyer. Pass directly to sales.
2. **Usage + no PQL**: Strong PQA but no buyer in user base. Marketing must find and connect the buyer (ABM).
3. **No usage + MQL**: Traditional top-down lead. Different playbook, different conversion rate, often different sales team.

## Self-Serve Monetization Framework

### The #1 Lever: Monetization Awareness

75% of freemium users don't know what you're selling. Before optimizing checkout flows or pricing tiers, fix awareness.

**Three pillars of free-to-paid conversion (in priority order):**

1. **Monetization awareness** (~80% of the work)
   - Feature walls, usage walls, trial nudges
   - Rule of three: users must encounter paid triggers at least 3 times
   - At SurveyMonkey, consistent gold-colored UI across all paid triggers + ensuring 3 exposures per user lifted conversion significantly
   - Track: pricing page views per activated account; run surveys asking "Do you know what we're selling?"

2. **Conversion friction reduction**
   - Pricing page optimization, checkout flow, currencies, payment methods

3. **Monetization model design**
   - Which features in which plan, price points, upgrade paths, add-ons
   - Last to touch because it's the most cross-functional

**Design review rule**: Review every new paid feature from EVERY customer state — free, lower paid, and target tier. If free users can't even see it exists, monetization awareness is zero.

### Benchmarks

| Metric | Typical range |
|---|---|
| Freemium → paid conversion | ~5% |
| Trial → paid conversion | 10-15% |
| Time from first sign-up to enterprise contract | 12+ months |

## Growth Model Evolution

### The 18-Month / 5-Year Rule

- **Every 18 months**: Introduce a new growth loop or channel (most will fail)
- **Every 5 years**: A major new growth engine must take significant volume share
- **Allocate 20-25% of growth team time annually** to exploring new loops (not goaled on immediate metrics; let them evolve for 6-18 months before adding metric expectations)

Most growth loops produce meaningful results for 5-7 years, then exhaust. Exception: Dropbox sharing loop, 17+ years and still firing — but that's an anomaly.

### Growth Loop Menu (Earned Channels)

| Loop type | Example | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Sharing / virality** | Dropbox file sharing (50%+ of acquisition) | Products with inherent send/share actions |
| **Invitation / collaboration** | Miro board invites, Figma project sharing | Team-based products |
| **User-generated content** | Miro's Miroverse template library | Products where users create public artifacts |
| **Referrals** | B2C: incentivized referral programs; B2B: "share with manager" report generation | Most products |
| **Community** | Community as engagement + acquisition engine | Products with active practitioners |
| **Word of mouth** | Product so good people can't stop talking about it | Products with "wow moment" potential |

## AI-Era Growth (2025+)

From Elena's experience as Head of Growth at Lovable ($200M ARR in <1 year, 100 people):

### What Changed

| Traditional playbook | AI-era playbook |
|---|---|
| 5% innovating, 95% optimizing | 95% innovating, 5% optimizing |
| Growth team optimizes surfaces | Growth team ships core features (Shopify integration, voice mode) |
| Activation is growth team's domain | Activation embedded in agent/product team |
| SEO is organic strategy | Social is organic strategy (founder-led, employee, customer) |
| Minimum Viable Product | Minimum Lovable Product |
| Aha moment | Wow moment |
| PMF is durable for years | PMF treadmill: recapture every 3 months |
| Adjacent user expansion | Constantly recapturing pioneers (latent majority risk) |

### AI Growth Levers

1. **Build in public**: Ship daily, talk about it constantly. Shipping velocity = noise = resurrection strategy.
2. **Founder-led socials**: CEO/team posting authentically on X/LinkedIn. Must show personality, not corporate copy.
3. **Influencer marketing**: 10x bigger than paid social for Lovable. Video demos show capabilities better than written value props.
4. **Give product away aggressively**: Treat LLM costs on freemium/giveaways as marketing spend, not COGS. Sponsor hackathons, give credits freely. Remove barrier of entry.
5. **Community**: Discord community with hundreds of thousands of members. Amplifies word of mouth and retention.
6. **Design as moat**: When functionality commoditizes, emotional experience differentiates. Brand lives in product interactions, not in brand marketing team.

### The PMF Treadmill

In AI, both product capabilities (new LLM releases) and consumer expectations shift every ~3 months. Companies must constantly recapture PMF, not just expand to adjacent users. This requires teams capable of BOTH finding PMF and scaling — simultaneously.

## Favorite Growth Frameworks (Reference)

1. **Growth Loops** (Brian Balfour, Casey Winters, Andrew Chen / Reforge) — Action → reaction → generates new action. Self-contained flywheels, not funnels.
2. **Race Car Framework** (Lenny Rachitsky + Dan Hockenmaier) — Engines (loops), fuel (paid spend), turbo boosts (events/launches), lubricants (optimizations).
3. **Adjacent User Theory** (Bangaly Kaba) — Expand PMF by optimizing for users just outside your core ICP.

---

## Key Principles

- **Growth amplifies, it does not create.** Never hire growth to find PMF or reverse decline.
- **There are patterns and frameworks, but no shortcuts.** 15+ years of experience confirms this.
- **Earned channels > rented channels.** Build what competitors can't buy.
- **Progress over perfection.** Velocity of information beats precision of measurement.
- **Hire advisors.** Run a paid workshop first, evaluate monthly, keep or replace. Any growth team without advisors is underperforming.
- **Your problem is not unique.** Someone has solved it. Find them and ask.
- **Product must own monetization.** In PLG/PLS, product can no longer be a feature factory that throws things over the fence to sales.
